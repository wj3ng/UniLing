Contests

Linguistics contests are a good way to prove your puzzle solving ability under a timed environment. The main linguistics contest for American high school (and middle school) students is the North American Computational Linguistics Olympiad (NACLO for short). The top finalists of NACLO qualify for the International Linguistics Olympiad (IOL), the most prestigious linguistics competition for high schoolers (and middle schoolers).

North American Computational Linguistics Olympiad

NACLO was first organized in 2007 to select US and Canadian students to go to the IOL. There are many contest sites around the continent. The nearest site from Uni High is UCI. The current format hosts an Open Round in January, which any student can participate in for free. The top 10% scoring participants in the continent qualify for the Invitational Round in March. The top eight US students and top four Canadian students get to represent their countries at the IOL, which takes place in a different host country every summer.

Each round has eight problems of varying difficulty, and the invitational round problems will generally be more challenging. Participants are given three hours in the open round and four hours in the invitational round. NACLO features problems that are more orientated towards computational lingusitics than its overseas and international counterparts. Since the contest is aimed towards English speaking students, there will be some problems that require some English knowledge (any native speaker should know enough English for this contest).

More information can be found on NACLO's official website and their student handbook.

International Linguistics Olympiad

The IOL is the most prestige linguistics competition for high schoolers (and middle schoolers) around the world. Each country (or territory) can send up to two teams of four students to a different host city every summer. There is an individual contest and a team contest. The top 1/2 of the participants get an honorable mention. For the individual contest, top 1/3 gets a bronze medal, top 1/9 gets a silver medal, and top 1/27 wins gold. For the team contest, the top three teams win gold, silver, and bronze.

In the individual contest, participants are given six hours to solve five extremely challenging problems. These problems tend to focus less on applied linguistics (including computational linguistics) than NACLO, and puts an emphasis on structure-focused linguistics (syntax, semantics, number systems, writing systems, etc.). In the team contest, each team is given four hours to solve one extremely challenging problem. This problem usually would need a lot of processing, so distributing work among teammates is necessary to get a good result.

More information can be found on IOL's official website.
